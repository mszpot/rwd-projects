Marie Curie

(1867 ― 1934)

Her research on radioactivity was a scientific breakthrough, earning her two Nobel Prizes in Physics and Chemistry. She was not only a brilliant scientist, but also a modest, generous and brave person who overcame social conventions that were unfavourable to women at the time of her life.

Early life

Marie Curie (née Maria Skłodowska) was the first person to win two Nobel Prizes, and the first woman to win a Nobel Prize. Born in Warsaw, Poland, in 1867 she was unable to pursue official higher education in her country due to her being a woman. After high school she studied at the Flying University, an underground educational organization. In late 1891 she moved to Paris, where she earned two degrees in physical and mathematical sciences.

The First Nobel Prize

In 1895 she married Pierre Curie, a fellow scientist, and took French citizenship. Researching radiation, a phenomena discovered by Henri Becquerel, they discovered two new elements - polonium and radium. Maria and Pierre Curie, together with Henri Becquerel won the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1903. Marie was the first woman to win such an award.

The Second Nobel Prize

In 1906, after her husband's tragic death, Marie was offered a position at the University of Paris, becoming its first female professor in history. She studied radioactive compounds and developed a process to isolate pure radium, which earned her the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1911. Marie was the first person to win two Nobel Prizes. She decided not to patent the process of radium isolation to facilitate further research for other scientists.

"I am one of those who think like Nobel, that humanity will draw more good than evil from new discoveries."

― Marie Curie

World War I

During World War I Marie Curie helped save the lives of thousands of wounded soldiers by setting up radiological stations and donating mobile X-ray ambulances called the petites Curies ("little Curies") to the army. She also conducted radiological training for lab assistants and medical students. Marie even used her Nobel Prize money to invest in war loans, not expecting or wanting to ever get it back.

Post-war years

Post war, Maria Curie continued her research on the chemistry of radioactive substances and their medical applications. Radium Institute (now Curie Institute) founded and led by Maria saw the rise of four more Nobelists, including her own daughter, Irène. In 1925, with Curie's help, a similar institute was opened in Warsaw. Maria Curie died in 1934 from aplastic anemia believed to be caused by exposure to radiation.
